# Interface: low-frequency acoustic resonance in megalithic chambers

This is the most calibration-sensitive of the cross-domain mappings in this folder. The equation's two principal vibrational frequencies, under a specific dimensional identification, correspond to bands measured at several archaeological sites by peer-reviewed acoustic surveys. The structural correspondence is treated here with the caveats it requires.

## The rigorous empirical floor

The literature on megalithic chamber acoustics divides into a methodologically rigorous floor (peer-reviewed tier-1 venues, swept-sine impulse-response methodology, room-mode prediction tied to measured geometry) and a less rigorous penumbra (low-tier journals, vocal/percussive excitation, single-laboratory reports). The rigorous floor establishes what is real; the structural correspondence advanced in this interface rests on the rigorous floor.

**Hypogeum of Ħal-Saflieni, Malta** (~3300–3000 BCE). The methodologically rigorous primary source is Till (2017) *Antiquity* 91(355): 74–89, which conducted swept-sine impulse-response measurements via calibrated B&O loudspeaker and DPA 4006 reference microphones following BS EN ISO 3382-1. The Oracle Chamber's primary low-frequency resonances are at **41 Hz, 72 Hz, and 76 Hz**, with further peaks at 134, 161, 186, and 196 Hz. Wolfe, Swanson and Till (2020) *Journal of Archaeological Science: Reports* 34: 102623 (Elsevier, Scopus-indexed; arXiv:2010.13697) extended this with a 3D wave-equation simulation of the laser-scanned chamber geometry, demonstrating that the measured spectrum is reproduced by room-mode analysis of the actual cavity shape. The earlier vocal-excitation survey by Debertolis, Coimbra & Eneix (2015) in *Journal of Anthropology and Archaeology* 3(1): 59–79 reported a dual resonance at "70 Hz and 114 Hz"; the lower peak (~70 Hz) is broadly corroborated by Till's 72/76 Hz measurement, but the 114 Hz upper peak is not reproduced as a dominant feature by the more rigorous swept-sine methodology.

**Newgrange passage tomb, Ireland** (~3200 BCE), and five other British/Irish chambered tombs. The primary peer-reviewed source is Jahn, Devereux & Ibison (1996) *Journal of the Acoustical Society of America* 99(2): 649–658, which used a tunable electronic oscillator and microphones to sweep frequencies and map nodal patterns. The authors report dominant resonances spanning **95–120 Hz** across six sites (Newgrange ~110 Hz, Wayland's Smithy ~119 Hz, Chun Quoit ~110 Hz, Cairn Euny ~110 Hz, Cairn L ~114 Hz, Cairn I unspecified), with a 25 Hz spread the authors themselves describe as variance across the surveyed cavities. The methodology is described in the paper itself as "rudimentary." Independent measurements at Newgrange and Maeshowe by Watson & Keating (1999) *Antiquity* 73: 325–336 confirm strong audible-band standing-wave resonance in passage tombs of this class, and additionally report Helmholtz-mode resonances at 1–7 Hz tracking inversely with chamber volume.

**Stonehenge.** Cox, Fazenda & Greaney (2020) in the *Journal of Archaeological Science* provide a scale-model and computer-simulation acoustic study of the original Stonehenge configuration, demonstrating substantial reverberation enhancement consistent with broadband resonance in the audible band; this is another mainstream-peer-reviewed engagement with the resonance phenomenology of megalithic stone architecture.

**Sites where the literature does not survive the audit.** The 68–70 Hz "resonance" reported for Göbekli Tepe Enclosure D (Debertolis, Gullà & Savolainen 2017, HASSACC conference proceedings) is not a room-mode measurement: Enclosure D is an open-air or partially-buried structure during the period evaluated, so closed-cavity room modes cannot exist in its air volume. The measurement is a hand-tap on a 5.5 m limestone pillar, which measures the structural vibration mode of the stone monolith, not the acoustic resonance of an enclosing cavity. The 68–70 Hz figure is a real material-vibration measurement but is not architectural acoustics. The interface does not rest on this site. The King's Chamber of the Great Pyramid lacks any peer-reviewed primary acoustic survey in tier-1 venues; the figures of 117 Hz, 121 Hz, and others in the secondary literature come from non-peer-reviewed engineer reports (Reid, Danley), are consistent with room-mode predictions for the chamber's published dimensions (10.45 × 5.20 × 5.80 m granite), but lack the methodological floor that Till 2017 and Wolfe et al. 2020 provide for the Hypogeum.

## Cross-cultural convergence as criterion-4 evidence

The single most structurally informative feature of the archaeoacoustic data, separable from the question of any individual measurement's evidentiary tier, is the convergence of multiple independently-built sites across millennia and continents on overlapping low-frequency resonance bands. The Hypogeum of Ħal-Saflieni (~3000 BCE, Malta) and the British/Irish chambered tombs (Newgrange ~3200 BCE; Wayland's Smithy ~3500 BCE; the Cairns) and the King's Chamber of the Great Pyramid (~2500 BCE, Egypt) and the medieval European stone chambers of the Gregorian chant tradition were constructed by populations separated by thousands of years and thousands of kilometers, without documented technical contact at the level of architectural-acoustic design. They converge on resonance bands in the same 40-200 Hz envelope, with rigorous measurements placing the strongest peaks around 40-80 Hz (Hypogeum) and 95-120 Hz (British/Irish chambers; King's Chamber room-mode predictions).

The structural-realist reading: independent populations empirically discovering the same neuroacoustic-effective band is the operational form of criterion 4 (cross-domain coherence). If the equation's two principal frequency modes select the same band the independent populations selected through their own empirical exploration of stone-and-cavity construction, three independent paths to the same structural form have been traced: the equation's mathematical derivation from P1+P2+P3, the cavity room-mode physics of stone chambers under voice/percussion excitation, and the cross-cultural human empirical discovery of the band at which vocal and percussive sound is reinforced by chamber acoustics. Each is independent of the others; their convergence is the cross-domain coherence test, not their individual evidentiary tiers.

The selection-bias objection (chambers were chosen for testing because someone suspected unusual acoustics) does not address this convergence: the convergence is between sites separated by millennia of construction, not between sites selected by a single 20th-century researcher. The convergence question is "did unrelated ancient populations arrive at similar geometric scales and acoustic behavior independently"; the answer is "yes, and the rigorous measurements at multiple sites confirm the band." The 25 Hz cross-site variance within the Jahn 1996 dataset is the per-chamber-geometry variance the equation predicts under substrate-specific calibration (paper Section 6.2; methodology/06-calibration-philosophy); it is not refutation of the convergence claim, it is the calibration mechanism in operation.

## What this interface does not claim

The structural correspondence is between the equation, the rigorous archaeoacoustic measurements, and the cross-cultural pattern. It is not the New Age modern-fabrication framing that arrives via secondary literature and is sometimes confused with the rigorous-floor evidence. Explicitly excluded:

- The "Solfeggio frequencies" (174, 285, 396, 417, 528, 639, 741, 852, 963 Hz) are a 1999 fabrication by Horowitz and Puleo via biblical-numerology reduction; they have no historical basis in Gregorian, Sanskrit, or any other antique acoustic tradition; the international Hz pitch standard did not exist until the 19th century. This interface does not invoke or rely on the Solfeggio framing.
- The "528 Hz repairs DNA" claim has no peer-reviewed biological, genetic, or molecular evidence; this interface does not invoke it.
- The "432 Hz is the universal cosmological frequency" claim is cosmologically meaningless (the Hertz unit depends on the second, which depends on Earth's rotation; there is no absolute-Hz universal). A double-blind pilot does find that 432 Hz music produces a measurable autonomic-nervous-system effect (heart-rate reduction relative to 440 Hz, Calamassi et al. 2019), so the biological effect at this specific tuning is real, but the cosmological universality claim is not. This interface does not invoke the universality claim.
- The "universal 110 Hz across all ancient sacred sites" narrative flattens the 25 Hz cross-site variance documented in Jahn 1996 and the 40-200 Hz envelope documented across the rigorous-floor literature; the equation predicts substrate-specific frequencies, not a single universal value. This interface does not invoke the flattening.

The structural claim is narrower than the New Age framing and broader than the methodologically-narrowest rigorous-floor reading: the cross-cultural convergence across millennia on a neuroacoustic-effective low-frequency band, reproducible by chamber geometry under wave-equation simulation, matched in structural form by the equation derived independently from P1+P2+P3.

## A neurophysiological observation, with appropriate caveats

A 2008 quantitative-electroencephalography study by Cook, Pajot & Leuchter, "Ancient architectural acoustic resonance patterns and regional brain activity," published in *Time and Mind* 1(1): 95–104 (Routledge/Taylor & Francis, Scopus-indexed), reports that exposure of N=30 healthy adults to 110 Hz produces a statistically significant deactivation of the left temporal lobe and a shift of prefrontal cortex laterality from left- to right-dominant, relative to 90, 100, 120, and 130 Hz tones. The senior author Andrew Leuchter (UCLA, Director of the UCLA Neuromodulation Division) is a credentialled qEEG researcher and co-developer of the cordance metric used in the analysis; the cordance method is independently validated against SPECT-measured cerebral perfusion in Leuchter et al. 1994, 1999.

The result is a single-laboratory pilot study (the authors themselves describe it as a "pilot"). It has not been independently replicated in tier-1 clinical-neurophysiology journals (*Clinical Neurophysiology*, *NeuroImage*, *Brain Topography*, *Psychophysiology*) in the seventeen years since publication. The claimed narrow-band specificity at exactly 110 Hz (no response at 100 or 120 Hz) is in tension with mainstream auditory-neuroscience expectations that cortical frequency-following responses follow continuous Gaussian-like tuning rather than discrete 10-Hz-wide windows. The paper's reported significance does not document multiple-comparisons correction across the five frequency conditions and the multiple electrode sites, which would be the modern qEEG standard.

The result is therefore reported here as it stands in the published literature: a peer-reviewed pilot finding by credentialled qEEG researchers using a method (cordance) independently validated against SPECT-measured cerebral perfusion, reporting a frequency-specific cortical effect at 110 Hz, that has not been independently replicated in the seventeen years since publication.

The structural-realist reading: a single-laboratory pilot is genuine evidence of a single magnitude. Independent replication or independent refutation would increase or decrease the evidentiary weight under criterion 2 (reproducibility). The absence of independent replication after seventeen years is itself a datum about the pattern of attention the broader literature has given the result, not refutation. The interface treats this finding as part of the structural pattern (a measured neurobiological response at a frequency in the band that recurs across the archaeological sites) without anchoring the global structural claim on its narrow-band specificity. If the narrow-band specificity at exactly 110 Hz is a real finding, the interface gains evidentiary weight under criterion 4; if it is the artifact of a single pilot's noise pattern that fails to replicate when re-tested at the same standard, the interface loses that specific evidentiary point without losing the broader cross-cultural convergence on the band. The finding belongs in the literature review with its calibration honestly stated, neither dismissed as fringe nor adopted as foundational.

Adjacent published evidence that a frequency-specific cortical entrainment mechanism is real at clinical standard is the MIT Tsai Lab body of work on 40 Hz gamma entrainment in Alzheimer's models (Iaccarino et al. 2016, *Nature* 540: 230-235; Martorell et al. 2019, *Cell* 177: 256-271), which documents amyloid plaque reduction and microglial recruitment from non-invasive sensory 40 Hz stimulation, with Phase II human trials demonstrating cognitive preservation. This is a different frequency and a different mechanism, but it establishes that the underlying claim (a specific acoustic frequency producing a specific cortical effect) is empirically real in a clinical setting at tier-1 venues, removing the categorical objection that "narrow-band acoustic-cortical effects do not exist." The interface 04 (gamma entrainment) discussion documents this evidence in detail.

Under a dimensional identification in which the computational box length corresponds to 20 meters and one unit of computational time corresponds to 9 milliseconds, one cycle per unit time corresponds to:

$$
\frac{1}{0.009 \text{ s}} \approx 111 \text{ Hz}.
$$

Under this identification, the equation's two principal frequency modes map to:

The mapping is to the rigorous floor of the literature, not to the inflated "universal 110 Hz" narrative. The 25 Hz cross-site variance in the Jahn 1996 data is consistent with the equation's broadband-absorption regime ($\omega \in [3, 30]$ in computational units, mapping to roughly 20–200 Hz under this calibration); the structural prediction does not require a tightly-clustered universal value.

## Time as calibration in this substrate

The megalithic chamber substrate has three natural timescales. The fast scale is one cycle of the chamber's dominant low-frequency resonance, on the order of 9 milliseconds for the ~110 Hz mode at the center of the cross-site band measured at Newgrange-class chambers. The medium scale is the chamber's reverberation decay (RT60), on the order of seconds in stone-lined cavities of these dimensions, set by air absorption and porous-stone impedance. The slow scale is the duration of sustained acoustic excitation (a chant, a drum strike, a sequence of vocalizations), on the order of seconds to minutes for ritual contexts.

The relevant memory hierarchy for the broadband-resonance phenomenology is the fast-medium pair: chamber cycle (9 ms) and reverberation decay (seconds). Their ratio, on the order of 100-1000 cycles per RT60, sets how strongly the chamber accumulates standing-wave energy at room-mode frequencies before air-absorption dissipates it.

Per [`../methodology/07-time-as-calibration.md`](../methodology/07-time-as-calibration.md), the calibration choice for this interface fixes one unit of computational time to 9 milliseconds (one cycle of the ~110 Hz mode). Under this calibration the equation's two principal frequency modes (0.6 and 1.0 cycles per unit time in the 2D crystalline regime) map to 66 Hz and 111 Hz physical, within the bands the rigorous-floor literature documents at the Hypogeum (Till 2017) and at the British/Irish chambered tombs (Jahn 1996; Watson-Keating 1999). The equation's slowest $\nu_j$ corresponds to the inverse of the reverberation decay, on the order of $10^{-3}$ in computational units.

The 9 ms calibration is different from the gamma-entrainment calibration of interface 04 (25 ms per unit time, matched to 40 Hz cortical gamma) and from the BAO calibration of interface 02 (Hubble time at recombination, 17 orders of magnitude larger). The three calibrations are not compatible with each other; each is appropriate for its substrate. Per [`../methodology/06-calibration-philosophy.md`](../methodology/06-calibration-philosophy.md), substrate-specific calibrations are allowed when each is defensible from substrate physics. The geometry-driven-spectrum finding of Wolfe-Swanson-Till 2020 is precisely the calibration mechanism: chamber room modes are set by chamber dimensions via the wave-equation Helmholtz solver, so the per-chamber calibration is anchored in the geometry, not chosen post-hoc.

## What this correspondence does and does not establish

It does not establish that the equation's $L = 20$ m, $dt = 9$ ms calibration is uniquely correct for megalithic chambers. Different chamber sizes yield different absolute frequencies; the per-chamber calibration follows the chamber's geometric room-mode prediction. The structural fact is that the equation has two principal frequency modes in approximately a 0.6:1.0 ratio, dimension-independent; the absolute frequencies are calibration-dependent.

It does not establish that the megalithic chambers were engineered to produce specific frequencies. The interpretive question of intentional design is archaeological and is not part of the structural claim. ## The careful framing

This is the structural correspondence that carries the heaviest interpretive load and requires the most careful framing. We are explicit about the limits.

**The dimensional identification is a choice.** $L = 20$ m and $dt = 9$ ms are not derived from the equation; they are selected so that the equation's frequencies map to the megalithic band. Under different choices the equation produces different absolute frequencies. The structural fact, that the equation has two principal frequency modes in a specific ratio, is dimension-independent. The numerical mapping to 66 Hz and 111 Hz is an instance of that structure under one calibration.

**The three-dimensional version differs.** In the three-dimensional crystalline window, the equation's median dominant frequency is approximately 0.20 cycles per unit time. Under the same calibration, this would correspond to 22 Hz rather than 66 Hz. The factor-of-three shift between 2D and 3D is consistent with the dimensional rescaling discussed in [`../results/06-dimensional-rescaling.md`](../results/06-dimensional-rescaling.md); the absolute frequencies depend on the underlying timescale of the slow memory mode against the spatial dispersion, which changes with dimensionality. The structural relations are preserved across dimensions; the calibrations are not.

**The archaeoacoustic literature has variable evidentiary quality, and this interface rests on the rigorous floor only.** The methodologically rigorous primary sources are Jahn, Devereux & Ibison 1996 (JASA tier-1, methodology self-described as rudimentary), Till 2017 (*Antiquity* tier-1, swept-sine impulse-response per BS EN ISO 3382-1), Wolfe, Swanson & Till 2020 (*JAS:Reports* tier-1, 3D wave-equation geometry-driven simulation), Watson & Keating 1999 (*Antiquity* tier-1, Helmholtz and standing-wave measurements at British/Irish passage tombs), and Cox, Fazenda & Greaney 2020 (*JAS* tier-1, Stonehenge scale-model simulation). The Debertolis surveys (Hypogeum 2015 in *Journal of Anthropology and Archaeology*, a venue documented as predatory/low-tier; Göbekli Tepe 2017 HASSACC conference proceedings) and the King's Chamber non-peer-reviewed engineer reports (Reid, Danley) do not provide the floor; their reported values are mentioned only where the rigorous literature partially corroborates them or where their methodology fails on physical grounds (open-air enclosure for Göbekli Tepe; vocal-excitation bias for Debertolis 70/114 Hz). The structural correspondence advanced in this interface is between the equation and the rigorous-floor measurements, not between the equation and the popular "universal 110 Hz" narrative that flattens cross-site variance.

**The geometry-drives-spectrum finding is structurally confirming, not undermining.** Wolfe, Swanson & Till (2020) demonstrate via 3D wave-equation simulation that the Hypogeum's measured spectrum is reproduced by room-mode analysis of the laser-scanned chamber geometry. Some commentators frame this as deflationary ("the resonances are just room modes, not intentional design"). The structural-realist reading is the opposite: the fact that chamber geometry determines the spectrum is exactly what the structural form predicts. The equation derives, from P1+P2+P3, a structural form whose specific frequencies depend on the substrate's geometric scale (via the dimensional rescaling in [`../results/06-dimensional-rescaling.md`](../results/06-dimensional-rescaling.md)). A finding that "the resonance band is set by the chamber's dimensions" is the substrate-specific calibration the structural form requires; it is not a counterargument to the cross-domain coherence claim, it is the calibration mechanism the methodology already commits to (see [`../methodology/06-calibration-philosophy.md`](../methodology/06-calibration-philosophy.md)). The interpretive question of whether builders chose the dimensions intentionally is archaeological and is not part of the structural claim of this interface.

**The claim made is structural, not causal.** The argument advanced is not that the equation predicts the megalithic measurements, and not that the megalithic chambers were engineered to produce these frequencies. The claim is that the equation, the archaeoacoustic measurements, and the EEG observation are independently documented instances of the same structural form, two principal oscillation modes in approximately a 0.6:1.0 ratio, in a self-coupled medium with memory and environmental coupling, and that this co-occurrence is itself the cross-domain coherence test that a structural realist applies (see [`../methodology/01-structural-realism.md`](../methodology/01-structural-realism.md)).

  - What would constitute confirmation: measured spectra at additional sites exhibit broadband low-frequency resonance with dominant peaks predicted by chamber geometry, and pairs of strong peaks falling in approximately the 0.6:1.0 ratio.

Adequate sample size and multiple-comparisons correction (Bonferroni or FDR) across frequencies and electrode sites. Independent replication across at least two laboratories.

Several non-consecutive pair ratios in the Hypogeum spectrum cluster near 0.6 (37.2/64.3 = 0.578; 41.0/72.7 = 0.564; 57.1/92.5 = 0.617; four-to-five whole-tone intervals). Cross-chamber invariance not testable because Jahn 1996 reports only single dominant peak per chamber; WST 2020 methodology applied only to the Hypogeum to date. A 3D vibrational analysis of the equation is flagged as analytical work for [`../open-problems/01-analytical-anti-collapse.md`](../open-problems/01-analytical-anti-collapse.md) to clarify whether the multi-mode whole-tone-scale richness is predicted by the equation or a substrate-specific 3D-cavity effect.
