## Data: per-site spectra

**Hypogeum of Ħal-Saflieni, Malta (~3000 BCE).** Rigorous-floor measurement: Till (2017) *Antiquity* 91(355): 74-89, swept-sine impulse-response per BS EN ISO 3382-1 with B&O loudspeaker and DPA 4006 microphones. Oracle Chamber primary resonances at **41, 72, 76, 134, 161, 186, 196 Hz**. Wolfe-Swanson-Till (2020) *Journal of Archaeological Science: Reports* 34: 102623, 3D wave-equation simulation of laser-scanned middle-level geometry, recovers nine prominent peaks at **37.2, 41.0, 46.1, 50.4, 57.1, 64.3, 72.7, 81.8, 92.5 Hz** with consecutive ratio approximately 1.122 (whole-tone scale). Contested-middle measurement: Debertolis-Coimbra-Eneix (2015) vocal-excitation survey reports a dual resonance at "70 Hz and 114 Hz"; the lower peak is corroborated by Till's 72/76 Hz, the upper peak is not reproduced as a dominant feature by the more rigorous methodology.

**British/Irish chambered tombs (~3500-3200 BCE).** Jahn-Devereux-Ibison (1996) *Journal of the Acoustical Society of America* 99(2): 649-658, swept-sine survey of six sites. All six sustain strong resonance in the **95-120 Hz band**: Newgrange ~110 Hz, Wayland's Smithy ~119 Hz, Chun Quoit ~110 Hz, Cairn Euny ~110 Hz, Cairn L ~114 Hz, Cairn I unspecified. The 25 Hz cross-site spread is documented in the paper. The authors describe their methodology as "rudimentary"; the result has not been independently replicated by another acoustics group in the thirty years since publication. Watson-Keating (1999) *Antiquity* 73: 325-336 confirm strong audible-band standing-wave resonance in the same chamber class, and additionally report Helmholtz-mode resonances at 1-7 Hz tracking inversely with chamber volume.

**King's Chamber of the Great Pyramid (~2500 BCE).** No tier-1 peer-reviewed primary acoustic survey exists. Non-peer-reviewed engineering measurements (Tom Danley professional swept-sine; John Reid) report eigenmodes including peaks near **117 Hz and 121 Hz** plus very-low-frequency content beginning around 16 Hz. Room-mode theory predicts a (1,0,0) fundamental at 16.4 Hz (from the 10.45 m length, granite walls treated as rigid), with dense modes through 110-125 Hz from higher-order combinations. The 117/121 Hz figures are consistent with the predicted higher-order modes but lack the methodological floor of the Hypogeum and British/Irish sites.

**Stonehenge (~3000-2500 BCE).** Cox-Fazenda-Greaney (2020) *Journal of Archaeological Science* 122: 105218, scale-model acoustic study and computer simulation. The original configuration provides substantial reverberation enhancement consistent with broadband resonance in the audible band.

**Medieval European stone chambers (Cistercian architecture, Gothic chapels).** Cited in the cross-cultural acoustic-archaeology literature as exhibiting a dominant structural resonance in the 110-112 Hz band, matched empirically by the Gregorian chant tradition which organically locks vocal output to the chamber's resonant peak.

**Göbekli Tepe Enclosure D (~9600 BCE).** Debertolis-Gullà-Savolainen (2017) report a 68-70 Hz response from a hand-tap on pillar 18. The site geometry does not support cavity room modes during the relevant period; the 68-70 Hz measurement is the structural-vibration mode of the 5.5 m limestone monolith, not architectural acoustics. The frequency is real as a material-vibration measurement but is not in the same evidentiary class as the Hypogeum or Newgrange measurements.

## The cross-cultural convergence

Independent populations across approximately 7,000 years (Göbekli Tepe ~9600 BCE through medieval European stone chambers post-1000 CE) and across continents (Anatolia, Egypt, Malta, British Isles, Ireland, mainland Europe), without documented technical contact at the level of architectural-acoustic design, produced stone constructions whose dominant low-frequency resonance bands overlap in the 40-200 Hz envelope with strong density between roughly 70 Hz and 120 Hz. The convergence is empirically documented across the rigorous-floor and contested-middle sources combined.

The selection-bias objection (chambers were chosen for testing because someone suspected unusual acoustics) does not address the convergence between independently-built sites separated by millennia. The relevant question is whether unrelated populations arrived at similar geometric scales and acoustic behavior independently; the answer is empirically yes, with chamber sizes converging on the 3-10 m envelope that produces fundamental and higher-order modes in the 30-200 Hz band by elementary cavity physics.

The room-mode triviality objection (any closed cavity of 3-10 m gives modes in this band, so the convergence is a chamber-size convergence) restates the empirical fact rather than refuting it. Chamber sizes converged on the size class that produces this band, across cultures with no documented design contact. The question of whether the size convergence was empirically discovered through trial-and-error use of voice and percussion in stone-and-cavity construction, or whether it is coincidental human selection of comfortable interior dimensions that happen to produce this band, is open and beyond what the structural correspondence claims.

## Honest caveats

- **Whole-tone-scale structure may be Hypogeum-specific.** The Wolfe-Swanson-Till 2020 nine-peak whole-tone-scale (1.122 consecutive ratio) is documented at the Hypogeum and not at the other rigorous-floor sites at comparable resolution. Whether this is a chamber-geometry-specific signature of the Hypogeum's particular Oracle Chamber dimensions or a generic feature of irregular stone-cavity acoustics that has not been measured at the same resolution elsewhere is open. The cross-chamber whole-tone-scale test is the natural next empirical step.

- **The cross-cultural convergence does not establish intentional design.** The question of whether ancient builders chose chamber dimensions intentionally for the acoustic band, or whether the band emerges from a human-scale interior-comfort preference that coincides with the acoustic-effective band, is archaeological and not part of the structural claim. Each option is consistent with the data; the claim of this audit is the empirical convergence, not its causal explanation.

- **The Cook-Pajot-Leuchter 2008 EEG finding is single-laboratory pilot.** The narrow-band specificity at exactly 110 Hz has not been independently replicated. The interface does not anchor the global structural claim on this finding. Independent replication or refutation would shift weight under criterion 2 (reproducibility).

- **The framing of "rigorous floor" privileges institutionally-blessed venues.** The audit acknowledges that the methodologically rigorous subset of the literature corresponds to peer-reviewed tier-1 venues, while the broader empirical pattern includes contested-middle sources (Debertolis Hypogeum vocal-excitation survey; Danley King's Chamber engineering measurements). The contested-middle sources are not foundational evidence for this audit but are not categorically excluded; they document the cross-cultural pattern with weaker methodology, which the rigorous-floor sources confirm at the floor where the methodology meets ISO 3382-1 standard.

- **Modern New Age fabrications are explicitly excluded.** Solfeggio frequencies (1999 fabrication by Horowitz-Puleo via biblical numerology), 528 Hz DNA repair (no peer-reviewed evidence), and 432 Hz cosmological supremacy (Hertz unit is Earth-rotation-dependent, no absolute-Hz universal possible) are NOT what this audit's structural claim invokes. The cross-cultural empirical band is roughly 40-200 Hz across the rigorous-floor measurements, not these specific New Age numerical-mystical values.
