# For neuroscientists

> The equation produces a crystalline regime with broadband absorption across 20–200 Hz under a defensible dimensional calibration — covering gamma, beta, and lower high-gamma — with a multi-timescale memory hierarchy structurally analogous to hippocampal–cortical consolidation.

$$
i\hbar\, \partial_t \Psi = \left[\,-\frac{\hbar^2}{2m}\nabla^2 + V_{\text{ext}} + \Lambda|\Psi|^2 + V_{\text{mem}} + \alpha(-\Delta)^{\sigma/2} - i\Gamma\,\right]\Psi + \eta
$$

with $V_{\text{mem}} = \sum_j \lambda_j y_j$ from auxiliary fields $y_j$ relaxing at rates $\nu_j$ — a multi-timescale memory hierarchy (fast $\nu=10$, slow $\nu=0.5$) structurally equivalent to the working-memory / long-term-consolidation pipeline.

## What this maps to in your area

Under a dimensional identification (1 unit = 25 ms), the equation's absorption band lands at 20–200 Hz, encompassing the 40 Hz gamma frequency at which Iaccarino et al. (2016) document amyloid-β clearance and the OVERTURE Phase II trial reports slowed cognitive decline. A separate calibration ($dt = 9$ ms) places the equation's two principal vibrational modes at 66 Hz and 111 Hz — matching Cook, Pajot & Leuchter's (2008) finding that 110 Hz acoustic stimulation (specifically 110 Hz, not 100 or 120) produces left-temporal deactivation and prefrontal laterality shifts. The equation's slow memory mode ($\tau = 2$) and fast mode ($\tau = 0.1$) correspond structurally to cortical consolidation and hippocampal CA3 — neither timescale was fit to biological data.
